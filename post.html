Part 2: Post employability profile

Q: Discuss providing 2/3 active experiences that you undertook during semester 1 as evidence using the STAR L/P model. Are there now any differences, has this experience impacted your personal and professional development. What changed because of this learning opportunity?

With the activities for the employability profile behind me, I can now reflect on the experience holistically and consider the broader influence on my personal and professional development. Over the course of the semester, I was able to engage with sixteen activities as part of this assignment, which gives me plenty to reflect on. But, to properly consider what changed over the course of this semester, I’m going to delve into a few specific activities & their influence on me. One of them will be the Flinders University game jam I participated in early on this semester. After that, I’ll discuss the CS Club Industry night, and how that affected my ability to engage with industry.

Game Jam

The first activity of the semester was the Flinders University game jam, which I summarise on this page. A couple times each year, Flinders’ Games Research Lab runs a game-making competition where you’re given a tight time limit, and asked to create a whole video game that fits a given theme. I was invited to join a team of game-making students that specialise in artistic direction and game design (focussing on user experience). I brought my computer science background, so my involvement was to be related to the technical & logical implementation of whatever the game would be.

It was a very much a team project. The four days of this competition were probably the closest and most collaborative I’ve ever had to work in a group project, and it required me to dramatically stretch my ability to work with people & communicate my thoughts. This began with the initial brainstorming session where we had to decide upon a direction for the game that matched every person’s strengths, abilities, and preferences. We each had compromise and consider a broad range of perspectives. After a discussion that lasted several hours, we had arrived at a logical puzzle game with a biblical theme. My primary task was to create an environment in C# that could process the interaction of each game ‘tile’ and create the programs for each of these ‘tiles’.

After helping with setup, and implementing the level rendering technology, I struggled a lot with the game environment for a full day or two. It took a while to find an approach that would work, but what eventually did it was sitting down with one other team member and talking through the problem aloud, breaking it down into its components (which element needs to communicate with which, the structure of the hierarchy et cetera). This was easily the part of the project that most necessitated I adjust the way I work with other people to be more communicative, lest the project not get done! With that hurdle passed, I made quick work of many of the individual tile programs. Then, towards the last hours of the project (literally), tragedy struck! Another tile was just not working! Once again, I broke down the logic step-by-step. This time, I realised that my existing implementation could not work under the existing paradigm. With only an hour left until the deadline, I presented my reasoning to my team, and we made the difficult decision to cut that tile, and adjust the later levels to not include it.

Nonetheless, the result was a project that I – and my team – were immensely proud of. I got to practice interpersonal skills in ways I hadn’t before, and now feel a lot more comfortable working in a team environment. Now, I no longer dread group projects, but look forward to them! The work created was leaps and bounds above what I could have created on my own, which is an important lesson to learn. It would seem other people thought our project good too – we won the coveted ‘Audience Choice’ award, and its associated prize, as voted by members of the public!

Industry Night

Another activity I was able to participate in this semester was the CS Club Industry Night. I provide a summary of this event here. Going into this assignment, one of my goals was to engage with employers directly. As I’ve discussed in the PRE section, I went into this semester without much in terms of a plan for post-uni employability nor, frankly, even knowledge of what opportunities were out there. The most important thing I could do during this Professional Practice assignment would be to change this, and try to learn as much as I can. Clearly, this thinking made the Industry Night a prime choice.

Going into the Industry night, I wanted to make sure I got the most out of it. I didn’t want to wander around aimlessly and come away with unspecific information that didn’t have much bearing on my life. Instead, I set myself the goal of talking to as many people as feasible, and coming back with a list of 3 places that not only could I apply for, but felt genuine excitement about the possibility. For me, the best way to engage is to make myself excited by a prospect, so this was of utmost importance.

When the night came, I began by doing an assessment of who was there and which places I’d like to hear more about. There were some I’d heard of, and some that intrigued me. I was initially nervous at the prospect of talking to them, but built up to it. I had some friends there with me that made it a bit less daunting. We started of by talking to the Department of Defence and hearing about some of the work they do, and what kind of students they’re employing. Gradually, I found it easier to engage with everyone there. One of my highlights was my discussion with Jane Street – they spoke of a really interesting internship opportunity based in Hong Kong. It’s very selective, but definitely something worth looking into. Another highlight was my discussion with Andrew Whitten from SAAB. As a recent graduate himself, I enjoyed hearing about his work relating to mathematical modelling. He told me about some of the SAAB internship & grad opportunities, which sounded particularly interesting in light of his work. I also broached the topic of ethical issues surrounding defence with Andrew and we had an enlightening discussion. His was a fairly nuanced perspective that alleviated some of my concerns regarding the industry.

Despite the fact that it was only a short 3 hours, I felt I had grown immensely and was far more comfortable & confident talking to the employers present. This became especially clear at the subsequent Career Expo which I approached with a renewed confidence. After the conclusion of the evening, I had a short list of places that I wanted to look further into and was excited by each one – which is exactly what I wanted! Before this assignment, one of my goals was to better my ability to network in unfamiliar settings. Another goal was to engage more directly with industry. I’d say that both of those goals were unequivocally achieved after this event. Personally, I noticed genuine growth, for which I’m very proud.

Conclusion

Of course, all of the events I participated in this semester helped shape my personal & professional skills in innumerable ways. I feel more confident broaching topics of employment. I’ve grown as a leader, as a collaborator, as a student. I feel more comfortable networking and have more experience doing so. The Game Jam and Industry Night only serve to exemplify this. The Employability Profile has been a wonderful experience, and I am certain it’s consequences will be strong and lasting.
